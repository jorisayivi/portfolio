Goal

This project was conducted during the second year of my Master’s in Econometrics and Applied Statistics, given by the modeling team of LCL (a subsidiary of Crédit Agricole). In teams of four, each group worked on a different anonymized dataset, all extracted from the bank’s portfolio of professional clients.
Our objective was to develop a regulatory credit scoring model in compliance with the Basel framework (IRB approach). Specifically, we were tasked with modeling the Probability of Default (PD) over a 12-month horizon using a logistic regression. The score had to be interpretable and robust, since such models are used by the bank to assess client creditworthiness and to calculate capital requirements through Risk Weighted Assets (RWA).

Data Preprocessing

We worked on a dataset of 50,021 professional clients, with 34 explanatory variables covering both financial behavior and borrower characteristics. Data preprocessing included handling missing values, imputing categorical variables, removing low-quality records, and treating outliers.
Continuous variables were discretized using CHAID and CART algorithms. Categorical variables were grouped based on default rates. Highly correlated variables were either merged or removed to limit multicollinearity. Finally, the dataset was split into stratified training and test sets (70/30) to preserve the default rate distribution.

Modeling

We used logistic regression to estimate the probability of default over a 12-month horizon. Variables were added step-by-step based on their discriminatory power, using Cramér’s V and significance levels. The final model included 9 variables and showed strong predictive performance (AUC = 0.883 on the test set). To improve class separation, we built a scorecard and segmented clients into five risk classes using quantiles.

Risk Class Segmentation

Risk class segmentation is a core requirement from regulators for internal rating systems. It ensures that the score can be used for default probability calibration, risk monitoring, and capital calculation (RWA). In our project, we segmented clients into five risk classes based on their predicted scores. Our initial segmentation using the Fisher-Jenks algorithm did not meet regulatory expectations, such as a 30% minimum relative gap in default rates between classes and at least 1% of defaults per class. We therefore adopted a quantile-based segmentation, which ensured balanced class sizes and sufficient risk separation.

However, we noticed residual heterogeneity in the highest-risk class, likely due to unmodeled subgroups with distinct default behaviors. To address this, we extended the model by including binary indicators identifying these subgroups. These new variables were statistically significant and operationally relevant. This alternative specification, with 11 variables, maintained the model’s performance (AUC = 0.889) and improved class homogeneity. For the remaining heterogeneity, we applied a Margin of Conservatism (MoC) to adjust the estimated PD and reflect model uncertainty, in line with Basel III.

Delivery

Our project was the top-rated submission of the year by the LCL risk modeling team. We were invited to present our work at LCL's headquarters in front of their analysts and managers. The team particularly appreciated the way we extended the model to address heterogeneity within the risk classes, demonstrating both methodological rigor and a strong understanding of regulatory expectations.
